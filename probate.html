Probate Basics

Understanding Probate in Florida

If you're navigating probate for the first time, here's what you need to know about the process, your responsibilities, and where a cash sale fits in.

Step 1

Filing the Petition

Probate begins when someone files a petition with the circuit court in the county where the deceased resided. The petition asks the court to admit the will (if there is one) and appoint a personal representative to manage the estate. If there's no will, Florida's intestacy laws determine who inherits and who can serve as personal representative.

Step 2

Appointment of Personal Representative

The court issues Letters of Administration, granting the personal representative legal authority to act on behalf of the estate. This is the critical milestone — once you have Letters of Administration, you can sign contracts, sell real property, pay debts, and distribute assets. For real estate sales, you may also need specific court approval depending on the terms of the will.

Step 3

Creditor Notification Period

Florida law requires the personal representative to publish a "Notice to Creditors" in a local newspaper. Creditors then have 3 months (or 30 days from direct notice) to file claims against the estate. Selling the property during this window is allowed, but the proceeds are considered estate assets and must be used to satisfy valid creditor claims before distribution to heirs.

Step 4

Selling Estate Property

With Letters of Administration in hand, the personal representative can sell real property. A cash sale to Transition Properties can close in as little as 7–14 days once authority is granted. This is dramatically faster than listing with an agent, which typically takes 60–90+ days and requires the property to be cleaned, repaired, and shown to multiple buyers.

Step 5

Distribution & Closing the Estate

After debts and expenses are paid, the remaining assets (including proceeds from the property sale) are distributed to the beneficiaries named in the will or determined by intestacy law. The personal representative then files a final accounting with the court, and the estate is officially closed.

Important: This is general information, not legal advice. Every estate is different. We recommend working with a probate attorney for your specific situation.

Common Questions

FAQs About Selling Probate & Inherited Property

Honest, straightforward answers to the questions we hear most from families dealing with inherited real estate.

Can I sell inherited property before probate is complete?

You can begin the selling process immediately, but the deed typically cannot transfer to a buyer until the probate court appoints a personal representative with legal authority to sell real property. The good news is that we can prepare the offer and purchase agreement in advance, so closing happens as soon as that authority is granted. In some cases — such as when the property is held in a trust, has a beneficiary deed, or qualifies for Florida's summary administration — full probate may not be required at all. We recommend consulting with a probate attorney to determine which path applies to your situation.

How long does probate take in Florida?

A standard formal probate administration in Florida typically takes 6 to 12 months from the time the petition is filed. Summary administration — available for estates valued under $75,000 or when the decedent passed more than two years ago — can be completed in as little as a few weeks. The actual timeline depends on whether the will is contested, how many creditor claims are filed, and the court's schedule in your county. Selling the property to a cash buyer like Transition Properties is one of the fastest parts of the process — we can close within days of receiving court authorization.

Do all heirs have to agree to sell an inherited property?

It depends on how the property is held. If the estate is going through probate, the court-appointed personal representative generally has the legal authority to sell real property as part of administering the estate, even if individual heirs disagree (though court approval may be required). If the property was inherited outside probate — for example, through joint tenancy with rights of survivorship — all owners on the deed typically need to agree. When heirs can't reach an agreement, any co-owner can file a partition action in court to force a sale. We have experience working with multi-heir situations and can coordinate with all parties to reach a fair resolution.

Can I sell an inherited property as-is without making repairs?

Absolutely. Transition Properties buys inherited homes in any condition — from pristine to severely neglected. Roof damage, mold, foundation issues, outdated wiring, overgrown landscaping, houses full of personal belongings: none of it matters to us. We handle all repairs after closing. You don't need to invest a single dollar or hour into the property before selling. This is one of the biggest advantages of selling to a direct cash buyer instead of listing on the MLS, where buyers expect move-in-ready homes.

Who pays closing costs when selling an inherited property?

When you sell to Transition Properties, we cover standard closing costs — including title search, title insurance, and recording fees. There are no real estate agent commissions (we're not realtors) and no hidden fees. The estate receives the full agreed-upon purchase price. Any outstanding obligations tied to the property — such as back taxes, remaining mortgage balance, HOA dues, or liens — are paid directly from the sale proceeds at the title company during closing.
